Chapter 1

The Bad Equilibrium

Twenty years ago, India's public policy ecosystem was stuck in a vicious cycle...

There were no thinkers producing sound ideas for policymakers. No one spoke about public policy, so not enough people wanted to study it. There were no institutions to train experts, and even if they emerged, there were no jobs for them. The supply side and the demand side were both empty.

🔄
The Vicious Cycle: No ideas → No demand → No training → No jobs → No thinkers → No ideas. Could one institution break every link in this chain?

"Was it possible to break out of this vicious cycle? Could one institution play a part in every link of this chain? Yes, and that just blows my mind."

— The Takshashila Story
2

From Acorn to Oak

Nitin Pai

Co-Founder & Director

Started as a blogger writing The Acorn — named because "mighty oaks grow from an acorn." Unlike other armchair observers, he put skin in the game.

The journey began with blogs, then a group blog called The Indian National Interest, then a digital magazine called Pragati — India's first international relations magazine — and eventually the institution itself.

"Nobody's going to give us 200 million dollars to start a think tank in India. So we had to think differently. The internet as a metaphor and the internet as a technology allowed us to create an institution unlike anything else."

— Nitin Pai
The Bumblebee Effect

Physicists said the bumblebee can't fly. The bumblebee doesn't know this, so it flies. People said Takshashila's three-in-one model can't work. Takshashila didn't listen — it just flew.

Chapter 2

Why "Takshashila"?

🏛️

The ancient city — cosmopolitan, multi-disciplinary, at the crossroads of Persian, Greek, Indian, Chinese civilisations. Known for statecraft and the Arthashastra.

🔦

The lighthouse logo — a public good, non-rivalrous and non-excludable. And it's tilted slightly to the right, because Takshashila advocates right-of-centre economics.

"Takshashila — because we want to be anchored in India, but global in outlook. And Institution — because that's where they keep crazy people. But also because only institutions that outlive careers and lifetimes can create transformative change."

— Nitin Pai
🎯
Three-in-One Model: Think Tank (ideas like Brookings) + Policy School (training like Kennedy School) + Media (discourse like The Atlantic). In India, you need all three to break the vicious cycle.

Chapter 3

Theory of Change

How do you turn a vicious cycle into a virtuous one? Connect three things...

👥

Good People

Select motivated individuals through rigorous courses. Every single one of the 300 students per batch is a bet that India will get better.

💡

Good Ideas

Equip them with economic reasoning, strategic frameworks, and the art of thinking about policy — not indoctrination, but deprogramming.

🔗

Good Networks

Connect them to platforms, jobs, and opportunities where change can happen. Where else will you have a Colonel as a classmate?

"A good person alone is not enough. There's this famous story of the monkey who saw a fish drowning, so he hung it from a tree. Many things in public policy are counter-intuitive. Good people can end up making very bad mistakes."

— Nitin Pai
🎯
The Overton Window: Don't just talk to the government. Expand the range of ideas the public considers acceptable. When citizens understand why reforms matter, politicians will follow.

Chapter 4

The Policy School

The school was meant to launch in 2018. Then the Anna Hazare movement happened in 2011, and one person on Twitter asked: "You say Lokpal is wrong. What is the right way?" Millions wanted change but didn't know how the system works. The school launched in January 2012 — three months from concept to delivery.

📋

GCPP: Advanced Public Policy

12 weeks

Market failures, government failures, economic reasoning, governance frameworks. Delivered online with mandatory physical workshops. Now in its 42nd cohort.

Policy Analysis Economic Reasoning Governance
🛡️

GCPP: Defence & Foreign Affairs

12 weeks

Military officers discover positive-sum economics. Civilians learn alongside Colonels. First to teach the Arthashastra in a modern strategic context.

Geopolitics Arthashastra Strategic Studies
💻

GCPP: Technology & Policy

12 weeks

For technologists curious about governance. Network effects, AI policy, platform economics. How the information age changes everything.

Chapter 6

Values & Culture

Not five values on a wall — a mind map of 25 things across four layers. Organisational values (freedom, pluralism, tolerance). Institutional values (independence, non-partisanship, no foreign money). Research values (evidence-based, strong beliefs weakly held). And a code of conduct for public discourse (civil, reasoned, never chase analytics).

💬 Tank Scrub

Weekly internal sessions where the sharpest minds share 10-minute presentations. A 19-year-old can challenge a 90-year-old — but must bring evidence.

🪑 No Back Row

In meeting rooms, junior people don't sit in the second row. Everyone sits at the table. Everyone can challenge anyone.

"The constitution is not a holy book. It's a user manual — a manual that says how to run your nation-state. And like any user manual, you can update it."

— Nitin Pai
⚖️
Strengthening the Republic: In a democracy, 99% can vote to sacrifice the 1%. A republic says you can't. That distinction — rule of law over rule of men — is at the heart of what Takshashila stands for.
